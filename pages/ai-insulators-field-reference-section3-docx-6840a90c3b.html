Section 3: Basic Geometry & Layout Methods

3.1 Introduction to Layout Geometry

Layout or pattern development refers to unfolding three-dimensional shapes into flat patterns for fabrication and fitting. Industrial insulation layouts typically use three primary methods: Parallel-line development, Radial-line development, and Triangulation. Each method converts the geometry of a fitting or component into a flat, cut-ready pattern.

3.2 Parallel-Line Development

The parallel-line method applies to cylinders, elbows, and rectangular prisms. Lines are drawn parallel to each other, allowing direct transfer of dimensions from elevation views. Typical applications include two-piece elbows, straight tees, and pipe segments.

Field Notes:

Place the seam on the shortest side for efficiency.

Account for joint types such as groove, slip, or peined joints.

Avoid cumulative error when dividing arcs for layout.

3.3 Radial-Line Development

Radial-line development is used for conical or tapered shapes such as reducers and transitions. It relies on measuring true lengths from a common apex to create accurate flat layouts. Each generator line of the cone becomes a radial element in the pattern.

Steps:

Draw the cone elevation and determine the true length of its side.

Swing arcs from the apex to mark true lengths on the pattern base.

Divide the base circle into equal parts and transfer points to the pattern.

Figure 3-3: Radial-Line Cone Layout [Colour retained]

3.4 Triangulation Method

The triangulation method applies when both ends of a fitting differ in shape or size, such as eccentric reducers or offset transitions. The pattern is formed by creating a network of true-length triangles, each corresponding to surfaces on the fitting.

Procedure:

Draw both end profiles of the fitting and connect corresponding points with projection lines.

Find true lengths by constructing triangles perpendicular to the projection plane.

Transfer the true lengths to the pattern layout, connecting sequentially to form the complete shape.

3.6 Efficient Drawing & Field Sequencing
