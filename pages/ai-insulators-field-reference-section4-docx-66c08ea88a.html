Section 4: Pipe & Duct Layout Patterns

4.1 Overview

Accurate pattern development minimizes waste and ensures consistent insulation coverage. The same geometric logic used for metal jacketing applies to removable insulation covers. This section provides layout methods and field-ready dimensional tables for elbows, reducers, tees, wyes, and offsets.

4.2 90° Elbows and Segmental Bends

Elbows are the most common fittings in insulation layout. Layouts may be two-piece, three-piece, five-piece, or seven-piece depending on desired accuracy and radius. Each pattern begins with an elevation view and step-off divisions based on the number of gores.

Field Formula: Arc Length = π × Diameter × (Angle ÷ 360)

Use fewer segments for small diameters or tight-radius bends.

Increase the number of gores for large diameters to improve smoothness.

Ensure equal divisions between inner and outer arcs.

4.3 Reducers and Transitions

Reducers connect different pipe diameters and can be concentric or eccentric. Radial-line or triangulation methods are used depending on the taper angle. Eccentric reducers maintain one side flat for proper drainage or clearance.

Figure 4-2: Concentric and Eccentric Reducer Layouts [Colour retained]

4.4 Tees and Wyes

Tees and wyes are complex intersections requiring precise projection between the run and branch. Equal tees divide the main run at 90°, while wyes typically intersect at 45°. Layouts use parallel or radial projection from the intersection line.

Figure 4-3: Tee and Wye Intersection Layouts [Colour retained]

4.5 Duct Offsets and Goose Necks

Offsets are used to connect misaligned ducts or pipes. Geometry relies on rise/run ratios and accurate transfer of centerline dimensions. Goose necks consist of multiple offsets for smoother transitions.

Figure 4-4: Duct Offset and Goose Neck Layout [Colour retained]

4.7 Practical Tips

Label each piece with diameter, radius, and joint type before cutting.

Test-fit with paper or foil before transferring to metal.

Number gores sequentially to ensure correct assembly.

Use magnetic or digital protractors to confirm angles.
