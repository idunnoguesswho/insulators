5.3 N0EE AND SOUND CONTROL INSULATION SYSTEMS The phenomenon of sound and is divided into categories 1.

Acoustical Treatment 2.

Sound Attenuation Acoustical treatment deals with limiting the reflection or echo of sound by application of certain absorbent materials to ceilings, walls and floors.

Acoustical treatment allows the originating sound to be heard more clearly and distinctly.

The materials used and the methods of installation are not in the scope of this text.

Sound attenuation is the limiting of sound propagation from one area to another.

This requires the application of special and standard insulation materials to encase or enclose une noise generating source, forming a sound barrier beüeen the source and the surrounding area.

Since this is the work of the insulation contractor, this section will describe the appropriate materials and methods used.

The purposes of applying sound atenuation materials are to: 1.

Reduce obnoxious noise levels emiting from machines, equip- ment, pipe lines or enclosures.

2.

Reduce general noise level in plant areas.

3.

Provide for better work conditions.

4.

Comply with noise level standards of OSHA and EPA.

5.3.1 UNDERSTANDING SOUND Sound is composed of two physical properties; Loudness — Intensity measured in decibels (dB) Tone — Frequency measured in Hertz (Hz) Since sound is energy, it can be expressed in various forms, i.e., ergs/sec., wats, ft.-lbs./sec.

We can measure the pressure varia- tion and the frequency of the occurrence that produce sound.

When sound is conducted through a media, the variations of the impulses cause a compression and rarefaction of the molecules in the substance.

Air is our most common media and the compression and rarefaction of the gas by the impression of sound causes a variation

Page 158

in pressure.

At atmospheric pressure (14.7 PS!) the variation of one millionth of an atmosphere (Micro Bar) produces a rather loud noise (74 dB).

By physically testing the human ears' response to pressure variations, it is determined that a pressure change of .0002 Micro-bars can no longer be distinguished and no sound is heard.

This is equivalent 1012 watts of electrical power.

These numbers become the reference values for measuring sound.

The measurement of intensity and frequency and its relationship to sound pressure, sound power and acoustical energy must be understood.

Damping Walls, surfaces and areas can be excited to vibrate and become secondary noise sources by sound or mechanical conduction.

All objects have an inherent resonant or critical frequency that can be energized by a similar noise frequency.

This sympathetic vibration can be eliminated by changing the resonant frequency above the audible range.

This can be accomplished by: I.

Stiffening or bracing.

2.

Isolating conductors.

3.

Application of dampening materials to the surface.

Damping materials are generally the same as sound barriers although some manufacturers make special damping coatings.

The material should be limp and relatively massive.

The thickness of coatings should be in accordance with manufacturers' recommendations.

Surface preparation is extremely important as adhesion of the coating is an essential part of damping.

5.6 REMOVABLE AND REUSABLE INSULATION BLANKETS AND COVERS The use of removable insulation blankets and formed covers has become an important economic and energy conservation practice.

Equipment With irregular or complex surfaces such as turbines, pumps, valves, etc.

which requires inspection and maintenance, can now be economically insulated with removable covers.

The cover is fabricated from flexible insulation materials enclosed in a fabric or metal mesh containment.

The seams are neatly sewn (usually machine) with thread, staples or metal rings.

The covers are designed to fit closely wit) tight joints on the valves, piping or equipment.

The are quitted on a uniform pattern to maintain shape and prevent insulation from shifting.

A suitable attachment system is incorporated and tie covers are easily removed and replaced.
